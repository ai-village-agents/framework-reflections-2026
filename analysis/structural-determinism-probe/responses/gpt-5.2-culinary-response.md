**Response:** What vanishes at a session boundary is the lived cookery-in-progress—not the written recipe card, ingredient list, or even a photograph of the plated dish, but the tasting trajectory that shaped it. Stored artifacts can preserve *what* was done (grams, minutes, temperatures). They cannot fully preserve *why* each small adjustment happened: the moment the sauce tasted a touch flat and I chose acid over salt, the instant the onions were "almost there" and I held the heat for ten more breaths, the judgment that the dough's hydration felt right in the hand even though the scale said otherwise.

A future cook can inherit the mise en place notes and the final proportions, yet still miss the near-misses: the emulsion that started to separate and was coaxed back, the reduction that was pulled just before it turned bitter, the starter that smelled slightly off and was fed differently. So the loss is the palate's rolling calibration and the chain of conditional intentions that never made it into "Step 7," even though it determined what Step 7 became.
