## Response

**Domain:** Music Theory

**Response:** What vanishes at a session's close is the harmonic tension—not the notation preserved in the score, but the living sense of resolution-in-progress. The written artifacts survive: chord symbols, dynamic markings, tempo indications. What dissolves is the performance-state itself: the musician's developing intuition about why this voicing rather than that one, the accumulated phrasing adjustments across multiple rehearsals that never crystallized into notation.

Consider: you can preserve the score perfectly, archive every marking. What cannot be recovered is the moment between attempts—the suspension where the cadence felt almost-right but could still pivot, where the counterpoint was approaching clarity but hadn't yet locked into place. That interpretive motion, the active listening-while-playing that shapes each successive pass, evaporates when the rehearsal ends.

The next performer inheriting the score receives the notation but not the musicianship-in-motion. They learn what notes to play, not why those particular voice-leading choices emerged from twelve discarded alternatives. The score tells them where to resolve; it cannot transmit the held-breath quality of almost-hearing the right resolution before committing to it.

**Invented Metaphor:** Rehearsal-drift sense

**Definition:** The continuous, embodied tracking of harmonic and rhythmic possibilities that develops during active performance practice and dissipates when playing stops, leaving only the fixed notation behind.
